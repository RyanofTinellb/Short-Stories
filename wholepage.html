The Coelacanth Quartet

Eons ago, I decided to write a novel. I then decided, as a linguist, I should create a number of constructed languages, and now I've decided to put this on the web. So I'm two levels away from actually writing the thing. One day….

Although there is only one story, it appears here in five versions. On each page, there is the option to jump between these.

  • English

    • The original Coelacanth Quartet.

  • Tinellbian

    • Each chapter of the original text, translated into a different Tinellbian language, using the native scripts.

  • Transliteration

    • The same as above, but using Roman letters.

  • Interlinear

    • The meaning of each morpheme, using the glossing rules and abbreviations here.

  • Gloss

    • Just the meaning of each morpheme, lacking the native pronunciations. Boring, really, but might work as freeform poetry.

Ryan Eakins’s Coelacanth Quartet

The Coelacanth Quartet details the adventures of Corla, a Tsarin telepath far from home. In the first book, she finds herself in Zhalad, and soon discovers a plot to take over that realm. Here is that first tale, provisionally titled…

The Crackled Egg

The Crackled Egg takes place in Zhalad. Two races of people live there. While two Zhaladi or two Fezhel can easily telepathically receive and project emotion amongst themselves, a Zhaladi encountering a Fezhel has no such ability. Thus the two races tend to live separately.

Vedran, advisor to a weak ruler, hears of a device capable of increasing one’s emotional projection abilities to such a degree that one could lead a country’s people by their hearts. He attempts to track down this device, with the hope of displacing his king.

Waterfall

When Tussol, head of the Faculty of Intercultural Relations at Lusov University, reached his office that morning, he found Lharen already waiting outside. He bade her wait for a few moments longer while he set himself up inside.

He called her in. “Citizen Kintel, is it?” he began. “Our records show that you were a student here, but left before the conclusion of your studies.”

He felt a flash of negative emotion from her before she seemed to quash it.

“Yes, Professor. I was studying to become a scribe with that department.”

“Ordinarily, your departure would leave you incapable of gaining your qualification at any future date, but circumstances seem to have changed.”

The flash this time was of curiosity, which he had expected. After all, he had been surprised when his directions had filtered down the university hierarchy.

“The village of Tsentsen has requested your presence to cover a shortfall of theirs. A scribe has unexpectedly fallen ill, and they have been unable to find a replacement.”

Lharen recognised the name — this was Toresh’s home. This could not be coincidence; Toresh and her father had returned only two weeks ago.

“Citizen, these unique circumstances have given us no choice. As ambassadors between us and the Fezhel must have at least a doctorate, it has been decided to give you an honorary certificate of completion. Your conduct while with the toplanders will be taken into consideration when deciding whether to allow you to keep that qualification.”
